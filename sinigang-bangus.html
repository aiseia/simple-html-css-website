How to Cook Sinigang na Bangus: A Delicious Filipino Sour Soup Recipe

By: JJ Mariano


About the Dish


Sinigang na Bangus is a cherished Filipino comfort dish celebrated for its harmonious blend of flavors and vibrant, sour notes. At the heart of this beloved recipe lies tender and succulent milkfish (bangus), a staple fish in the Philippines. The fish is gently simmered in a fragrant broth that derives its distinctive tang from the use of tamarind or other souring agents like calamansi or green mango. This delightful sourness is balanced with an assortment of fresh vegetables, often including tomatoes, string beans, eggplants, and water spinach (kangkong), creating a colorful and nutrient-rich medley. Sinigang na Bangus captures the essence of Filipino culinary artistry, offering a warm, comforting, and invigorating dining experience that is cherished across the archipelago.

The sinigang broth, a cornerstone of the dish, is a masterpiece of contrasts - a tantalizing harmony of sourness, savory depth, and a touch of umami. Often enriched with fish sauce (patis) or shrimp paste (bagoong), it presents a complexity of flavors that tantalize the taste buds. Whether enjoyed on a rainy day to warm the soul or as a refreshing option during the heat of summer, Sinigang na Bangus embodies the heart and soul of Filipino cuisine, showcasing a deep connection to both tradition and nature through its use of fresh, locally sourced ingredients. This Filipino sour soup is not just a dish; it's a culinary journey that invites all who savor it to partake in the rich tapestry of Philippine flavors and culture.

Ingredients

  • 1-2 pieces of bangus (milkfish) belly or any preferred parts, cleaned and sliced into serving pieces
  • 1 medium-sized onion, quartered
  • 2-3 tomatoes, quartered
  • 2-3 pieces green chili peppers (siling haba), sliced diagonally (adjust to your preferred level of spiciness)
  • 1-2 pieces radish (labanos), peeled and sliced into rounds
  • 8-10 pieces string beans (sitaw), cut into 2-inch lengths
  • 2-3 pieces eggplants (talong), sliced into rounds
  • 1-2 bundles of water spinach (kangkong) or kangkong leaves, separated from the stems
  • 2-3 tablespoons fish sauce (patis)
  • 1-2 packets of sinigang mix (tamarind or calamansi flavor) or use fresh tamarind or calamansi juice for a more traditional approach
  • 8-10 cups of water (adjust based on your preferred soup consistency)
  • Salt and pepper to taste
  • Cooking oil
  • 2-3 cloves of garlic, minced (for sautéing)

Procedure

  1. If using sinigang mix: Follow the package instructions to dissolve the sinigang mix in a small amount of water. Set it aside
  2. If using fresh tamarind or calamansi juice: Extract the juice from the tamarind or calamansi. Set it aside.
  3. In a large pot, heat a small amount of cooking oil over medium heat.
  4. Add the minced garlic (if using), onion, and tomatoes. Sauté until the tomatoes start to soften and release their juices.
  5. Carefully add the bangus slices to the pot. Let them cook for a few minutes until they turn slightly opaque.
  6. If using sinigang mix, pour in the dissolved mix into the pot. If using fresh tamarind or calamansi juice, add it at this stage. Stir well to incorporate the souring agent.
  7. Add the radish, string beans, and eggplants to the pot. Stir to combine all the ingredients.
  8. Season the soup with fish sauce (patis) to taste.
  9. Start with a few tablespoons and adjust according to your preference. Remember that you can always add more later if needed.
  10. Add enough water to cover the ingredients and create the desired soup consistency. Typically, you'll need about 8-10 cups of water, but you can adjust based on your preference. Stir well.
  11. Bring the soup to a boil, then reduce the heat to a gentle simmer.
  12. Cover the pot and let it simmer for about 10-15 minutes or until the vegetables are tender.
  13. Once the vegetables are almost done, add the kangkong leaves or water spinach to the pot. Cook for an additional 2-3 minutes until the kangkong wilts.
  14. Taste the soup and adjust the seasoning with salt and pepper as needed. The soup should have a delightful balance of sourness and saltiness. Once it's to your liking, remove the pot from the heat.
  15. Ladle the Sinigang na Bangus into bowls and serve hot. It's commonly served with steamed rice and shrimp paste (bagoong) or fish sauce (patis) on the side for additional seasoning at the table.

Feel free to adjust the quantities of ingredients based on your preferences and the number of servings you intend to make. Sinigang is highly customizable, and you can make it as sour or as mild as you like. Now, enjoy your homemade Sinigang na Bangus, a Filipino favorite that's both comforting and refreshingly sour!
